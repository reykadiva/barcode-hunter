## 1. Reference Analysis

### 1.1 Rustical — Pixel-Art Gaming Platform

#### Visual Strengths

**Consistent pixel-art commitment**: Every element — characters, backgrounds, UI, particles — adheres to a unified pixel grid. There is no stylistic fragmentation. The pixel art is not a gimmick layered on top of a standard website; it IS the website. This level of consistency creates instant brand recognition.

**Atmospheric depth through color**: The deep purple palette (`#1A0A3A` base) creates a sense of infinite space. The lighter purple gradients and cyan particle effects generate depth without requiring complex 3D rendering. The entire scene feels like looking into a world, not at a webpage.

**Strong focal hierarchy**: The hero composition forces the eye through a clear path — headline → character → CTA → stats. The large pixel-art character riding the dragon is impossible to miss and immediately communicates "this is a game about adventure."

**Confident typography on atmospheric backgrounds**: The white sans-serif headings sit cleanly against the dark purple without competing with the pixel art. The stat numbers (40K, 15+, 24/7) use massive scale to communicate authority.

#### Weaknesses

**Hostile atmosphere for casual users**: The dark palette and aggressive pixel-art density create a "hardcore gamer" feeling that would repel the cozy-game audience. There is no warmth, no invitation, no softness.

**Static hero**: Despite the atmospheric background, the character and dragon are essentially a static image. For a game about living creatures, the absence of subtle idle animation is a missed opportunity.

**Information overload at first glance**: Navigation, headline, character, stats, scroll indicator, social icons — everything competes for attention simultaneously. The eye doesn't know where to rest.

#### Emotional Impact

Excitement. Adventure. Community. "This is a place where serious players gather." The emotional tone is energetic and masculine — appropriate for its audience, but the opposite of what Scan Chan needs.

#### What Makes It Premium

The unwavering commitment to a single visual language. Every pixel belongs to the same world. There are no compromises, no fallbacks to generic web patterns. The pixel art is not decoration — it is the medium.

#### What Should Never Be Copied

The dark, high-energy aesthetic. Scan Chan must feel like a warm room, not a dark dungeon. The density of visual information. Scan Chan must breathe.

#### What Is Worth Adapting

- **Principle of total consistency**: Every element must belong to the same world
- **Atmospheric color immersion**: Using color to create a sense of place rather than decoration
- **Confident focal hierarchy**: One dominant visual element per screen, with everything else supporting it
- **Pixel-art as identity, not decoration**: If pixel art is chosen, it must be the visual language of the entire product — not a style applied to some elements while others remain standard

### 1.2 Kuaishou AI Video Editor — 3D Character Landing

#### Visual Strengths

**Character as emotional anchor**: The 3D character in the spacecraft is the entire personality of the page condensed into one figure. Goggles, helmet number, posture, expression — everything communicates "this is fun, this is easy, this is for you." The character does the heavy lifting that copy and layout cannot.

**Warmth through color temperature**: The orange spacecraft against a muted blue-purple gradient creates a warm-cool contrast that feels inviting without being aggressive. The orange is energetic but friendly; the blue-purple is calming but not cold.

**Playful decorative language**: The doodle-style rockets, beach ball, and swirl lines add personality without demanding attention. They exist at the periphery of vision — noticed but not distracting. This creates a sense of a living, playful world.

**Pill-shaped navigation as modern pattern**: The dark rounded navigation pill floating at the top is a confident, modern pattern that feels approachable and unobtrusive.

**Asymmetric hero layout**: Text on the left, character on the right creates natural visual flow without centering everything. The asymmetry feels designed, not templated.

#### Weaknesses

**3D character may not age well**: Stylized 3D renders can feel dated within a few years as rendering trends shift. Pixel art and illustration tend to age more gracefully.

**Brand identity feels corporate-playful rather than genuinely warm**: The friendliness feels designed by a marketing team rather than organic. There's a subtle "we're trying to seem approachable" quality that differs from genuine warmth.

**Background gradient is generic**: The blue-purple gradient is a common SaaS landing page pattern. It works but doesn't create a unique sense of place.

#### Emotional Impact

Approachability. Fun. Simplicity. "This won't be hard." The emotional tone is welcoming and slightly whimsical — closer to Scan Chan's needs than the other references, but still lacking intimacy.

#### What Makes It Premium

The character design carries the entire emotional weight. The rest of the page is competent but conventional. The premium feeling comes from the character's quality and personality, which elevates everything around it.

#### What Should Never Be Copied

The corporate-playful tone. Scan Chan should feel genuinely personal, not brand-friendly. The blue-purple gradient as a default background.

#### What Is Worth Adapting

- **Character as emotional anchor**: A well-designed mascot carries the entire product's personality
- **Warm-cool color contrast**: Using warm accents against cool backgrounds to create inviting compositions
- **Playful peripheral decorations**: Small decorative elements that add life without demanding attention
- **Asymmetric hero composition**: Text and character sharing the stage without centering everything
- **Pill-shaped navigation**: Clean, modern, approachable navigation pattern

### 1.3 AdventureYo — Immersive Game Studio

#### Visual Strengths

**Cinematic immersion through illustration**: The fantasy landscape is not a background — it is the experience. The mountains, river, cliffside, adventurer, birds, and moon create a complete world that the player wants to enter. This is the most emotionally powerful technique in the reference set.

**Warm sunset color temperature**: The golden-hour palette (ochre, burnt orange, olive, deep brown) creates immediate warmth and nostalgia. This is the color of memory, of longing, of "I want to be there."

**Textural authenticity**: The painterly textures and torn-paper header edge create a handcrafted feeling that digital-clean designs cannot achieve. Imperfection communicates authenticity.

**Strong narrative copy paired with visual**: "Uncover a World of Hidden Treasures" over a landscape full of visual secrets creates alignment between word and image. The copy doesn't describe — it invites.

**Clear typographic hierarchy**: Despite the rich background, the text is readable and well-ordered. Headline, subheadline, CTA, and body text each have clear visual weight.

#### Weaknesses

**Landscape as hero is a one-time trick**: The immersive landscape works for a first impression but cannot sustain daily use. A game hub or pet room cannot be a full-bleed landscape painting every time you open it.

**No character relationship**: The adventurer in the scene is distant and small. There is no companion to bond with, no creature to care for. The emotional connection is to the world, not to a being.

**Static and heavy**: The page feels like a painting — beautiful but frozen. There is no sense of life, movement, or responsiveness.

#### Emotional Impact

Wonder. Longing. Adventure. "I want to go there." The emotional tone is epic and aspirational — powerful but not intimate. Scan Chan needs the warmth of this palette but at a much smaller, more personal scale.

#### What Makes It Premium

The quality of the illustration and the restraint in layout. The page doesn't try to be clever — it lets the art speak. The confidence to use a single powerful image instead of many competing elements.

#### What Should Never Be Copied

The epic scale. Scan Chan is intimate, not expansive. The distant character. Scan Chan's pet must be close, present, and personal.

#### What Is Worth Adapting

- **Warm sunset palette**: Golden-hour colors create immediate warmth and nostalgia — directly aligned with Scan Chan's VDD Honey/Cream palette
- **Textural authenticity**: Subtle paper grain, fabric weave, and organic textures add handcrafted warmth
- **Confidence in restraint**: Let one powerful visual carry the page instead of filling it with elements
- **Narrative alignment**: Every piece of copy should reinforce what the visual is communicating
- **Handcrafted edge details**: Torn paper, soft borders, and organic shapes instead of rigid rectangles

### 1.4 Heptic Arena — Cyberpunk Game Brand

#### Visual Strengths

**Bold, unwavering brand identity**: The red-black-yellow palette is aggressive, memorable, and impossible to confuse with any other brand. The identity is so strong that a single color swatch would identify the product.

**Character design as worldbuilding**: Each of the three characters tells a complete story through costume, species, posture, and equipment. The human, alien, and reptilian together communicate "this world is diverse, strange, and exciting."

**Dynamic composition**: The diagonal red lines and angular geometry create constant visual energy. Nothing is still. Even the static page feels like it's moving.

**High-contrast typography**: White and yellow text on deep red and black creates maximum readability with maximum drama.

#### Weaknesses

**Maximum intensity, zero rest**: The page never lets the eye relax. Every element is loud, bold, and competing for attention. This creates fatigue, not engagement.

**Hostile to the target audience**: The aggressive, masculine, combat-oriented visual language would actively repel the cozy-game audience.

**No warmth, no softness, no invitation**: Every element communicates challenge, competition, and intensity. There is no safe space.

#### Emotional Impact

Adrenaline. Excitement. Competition. "This is intense." The opposite of what Scan Chan needs — but the intensity of commitment is admirable.

#### What Makes It Premium

The confidence of the brand identity. There is no hedging, no trying to appeal to everyone. It knows exactly what it is and commits fully.

#### What Should Never Be Copied

Anything visual. The aggressive palette, the angular geometry, the high-energy composition, the combat aesthetic — all are antithetical to Scan Chan's emotional goals.

#### What Is Worth Adapting

- **Principle of absolute commitment**: Whatever visual identity Scan Chan adopts, it must commit fully — no hedging between styles
- **Character diversity as worldbuilding**: If Scan Chan has multiple pet types or NPCs, each should tell a story through visual design
- **High contrast for key moments**: Reserved for evolution events and achievements — not everyday UI

### 1.5 Cross-Reference Synthesis

#### Common Principles Across All References

Despite their differences, all four references share design principles that transcend genre:

| Principle | How Each Reference Uses It | Scan Chan Application |
|-----------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| **Consistent visual language** | Every element belongs to one world | Pixel art + warm UI must be one cohesive system |
| **Character as emotional anchor** | Mascot/figure carries brand personality | Scan Chan the cat IS the brand |
| **Confident restraint** | One dominant visual, not many competing | The pet is always the focal point |
| **Color as atmosphere** | Palette creates emotional environment | Honey/Cream warmth creates cozy safety |
| **Texture adds authenticity** | Organic imperfection vs sterile perfection | Paper grain, soft shadows, lived-in feeling |
| **Typography supports tone** | Font choice reinforces brand voice | Fredoka + Nunito = friendly and warm |

#### What None of the References Have

Critically, none of the references achieve what Scan Chan must achieve:

- **Intimacy**: All are designed for first impressions, not daily companionship
- **Warmth without energy**: All are either warm-and-exciting or calm-and-cold; none are warm-and-calm
- **Companion relationship**: All present characters to look at, not creatures to bond with
- **Slow beauty**: All optimize for immediate impact; Scan Chan must reward long attention

This gap IS the opportunity. Scan Chan's visual identity occupies a space that none of these references fill: **warm, calm, intimate, companion-centered**.

## 2. Scan Chan Identity

### 2.1 What Scan Chan Should Feel Like

Scan Chan should feel like opening a favorite storybook that has a living creature inside it.

**Core feeling words**:

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Cozy** | Wrapped in warmth, safe from the outside world |
| **Calm** | Nothing urgent, nothing stressful, time slows down |
| **Premium** | Handcrafted, intentional, every detail considered |
| **Warm** | Golden-hour light, honey tones, gentle radiance |
| **Modern** | Contemporary sensibility, not retro pastiche |
| **Soft** | Rounded, pillowy, no sharp edges anywhere |
| **Alive** | Always breathing, always responding, never static |
| **Lovable** | The kind of thing you want to protect and show to friends |
| **Intimate** | Personal, close, a world for one |
| **Nostalgic** | Feels like a happy memory even while you're in it |

### 2.2 What Scan Chan Should Never Feel Like

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails |
|--------------|-------------|
| **SaaS** | Corporate, productivity-focused, transactional |
| **Dashboard** | Information-dense, utilitarian, analytical |
| **Enterprise software** | Complex, overwhelming, feature-driven |
| **Generic mobile app** | Cookie-cutter, forgettable, interchangeable |
| **AI-generated UI** | Predictable patterns, no soul, no intention |
| **Flat material design** | Sterile, corporate, lacking warmth |
| **Information overload** | Cluttered, anxious, competing for attention |
| **Retro gaming pastiche** | Nostalgia without purpose, costume without substance |
| **Hyper-casual game** | Cheap, disposable, dopamine-driven |
| **Social media** | Scroll-driven, attention-hungry, comparison-inducing |

### 2.3 The Identity Statement

> Scan Chan is a **warm, intimate companion space** that uses **pixel-art charm** within a **soft, modern interface** to create a feeling of **cozy nostalgia**. It looks like a **storybook with a living creature inside** — handcrafted, loving, and personal. It is the **visual opposite of everything demanding your attention** on your phone.

### 2.4 Visual Identity Principles

1. **The pet is the brand.** The mascot IS the logo, the identity, and the emotional center. Everything visual orbits the pet.
2. **Warmth over coolness.** Every color decision defaults to warm. Cool colors appear only as rest, contrast, or night mode.
3. **Soft over sharp.** Every corner, every edge, every transition is rounded. Sharpness is banned.
4. **Life over polish.** A slightly imperfect, breathing element is better than a perfect, static one.
5. **Few over many.** One beautiful thing is better than ten adequate things. Restraint is a virtue.
6. **Story over feature.** Every screen tells a micro-story. "Your cat is napping in sunlight" is more compelling than "Hunger: 65%."

## 4. Mascot Redesign Strategy

### 4.1 Assessment of Current Prototype

The current pixel cat is a functional prototype. It successfully demonstrates:
- SVG-based pixel art rendering (24x24 grid)
- Variant system (8 color palettes)
- Action overlay system (scan gun, basket, gamepad, etc.)
- Basic animation (ear twitch, eye blink, laser pulse)

**What it lacks**:
- **Personality**: The cat is a generic pixel template, not a character with identity
- **Emotional range**: Only two states (default, action-holding) — no moods, no expressions
- **Silhouette**: Boxy, symmetric, no distinctive shape
- **Eyes**: 2x2 pixel blocks — no expressiveness, no soul
- **Proportions**: Equal head-to-body ratio — no cuteness amplification
- **Animation vocabulary**: Limited to ear twitch and eye blink
- **Evolution readiness**: No visual path from kitten to legendary

The current cat is a placeholder. The mascot redesign must create a character that becomes the emotional and visual identity of the entire product.

### 4.2 Personality Direction

Scan Chan the mascot should embody:

- **Curious** — always investigating, sniffing, watching
- **Gentle** — soft movements, no aggression
- **Slightly clumsy** (as kitten) → **Graceful** (as adult)
- **Expressive** — emotions readable from silhouette alone
- **Independent but affectionate** — a real cat's personality, not a cartoon dog's

The mascot should feel like a real cat that happens to be made of pixels — not a cartoon character that happens to be a cat.

### 4.3 Shape Language

**Overall silhouette**: Soft oval / egg shape. The cat should read as a rounded, compact form from a distance. No angular protrusions.

**Head-to-body ratio**:
- Kitten: 1:1 (oversized head = maximum cuteness)
- Young Cat: 1:1.3
- Adult Cat: 1:1.5
- Wise Cat: 1:1.5 (but with different proportions — longer body)
- Legendary Cat: 1:1.5 (with ethereal additions — glow, trails)

**Key silhouette features**:
- Distinctive ear shape (slightly rounded tips, not pointed)
- Compact, round body when sitting
- Tail as a key expressive element (long enough to be visible, curled at rest)
- Small paws visible when sitting
- Compact enough to be recognizable at 32x32px

### 4.4 Facial Proportions

**Face layout** (within the head area):
- Eyes positioned at the vertical center of the head (large, expressive)
- Small triangular nose below center
- Mouth implied, not drawn (except when eating/meowing)
- Cheek blush spots (optional, for happy states)
- Whiskers as 1px lines (subtle, removable at small sizes)

### 4.5 Eye Philosophy

The eyes are the most important element of the entire mascot.

**Principle**: If the eyes are right, the character is right. If the eyes are wrong, nothing else matters.

**Eye design direction**:
- Large relative to head size (pixel-art convention, but not exaggerated)
- 3x3 or 4x4 pixel area per eye at base resolution
- Two-tone: dark base + single highlight pixel (creates life/sparkle)
- Shape changes for expressions: round (neutral), half-closed (content/sleepy), wide (curious/excited), arched (happy/satisfied)
- Blink animation: quick close-open with slight anticipation

**What the eyes must communicate**:
- The cat is alive (constant subtle movement)
- The cat is aware (looking at the player, at objects, at movement)
- The cat has feelings (emotion changes the eye shape)
- The cat trusts the player (soft eyes, not alert/defensive)

### 4.6 Ear Movement

Ears are the cat's radar and emotional indicator:

- **Neutral**: Upright, slightly forward
- **Alert/Curious**: Both ears forward, slightly rotated
- **Happy**: Relaxed, slightly to the sides
- **Sleepy**: Slowly drooping backward
- **Startled**: Flat back (rare — should almost never happen in a cozy game)
- **Twitch**: Quick single-ear rotation (random idle animation)

### 4.7 Tail Movement

The tail is the cat's emotional semaphore:

- **Content**: Slow, gentle swish
- **Happy**: Upright with a slight curl, gentle wave
- **Excited**: Fast swish or upright quiver
- **Sleepy**: Wrapped around body
- **Curious**: Straight out, slight upward curve at tip
- **Focused**: Still, pointing at object of interest

### 4.8 Animation Personality

The mascot should have a rich animation vocabulary that communicates personality without words:

**Idle behaviors** (random, looping):
- Slow blink
- Ear twitch (single ear)
- Tail swish
- Yawn (when energy is low)
- Stretch (after waking)
- Grooming (licking paw)
- Looking around (head turn left/right)
- Settling (adjusting sitting position)

**Reaction behaviors** (triggered):
- Approach (when player opens app)
- Sniff (when new product appears)
- Eat (when fed)
- Purr (chin up, eyes half-closed, body vibrating slightly)
- Play (pounce, chase)
- Startle (small jump — only for rare surprises)
- Nap curl (transition to sleeping position)

### 4.9 Emotional Readability

The cat's emotional state must be readable from the silhouette alone, without seeing facial details:

| Emotion | Silhouette Change |
|---------|------------------|
| Happy | Upright, open, tail up |
| Content | Relaxed sitting, tail wrapped |
| Sleepy | Lowered, compact, head drooping |
| Curious | Leaning forward, ears up, tail out |
| Excited | Bouncy, stretched tall, tail vibrating |
| Sad | Compact, lowered, tail flat |

### 4.10 Pixel Art Direction

**Base resolution**: 32x32 or 48x48 pixel grid (up from current 24x24)

**Why larger**: The current 24x24 grid is too small for expressive eyes, detailed ears, and visible tail movement. A 32x32 or 48x48 grid provides enough resolution for:
- 3x3 or 4x4 eyes with highlight pixels
- Detailed ear interiors
- Visible tail with curl
- Body markings and patterns
- Subtle shading (2-3 tones per color)

**Pixel art style**:
- Clean pixel edges (no anti-aliasing within the sprite)
- Warm brown outlines instead of black (see VDD section 8.1)
- 2-3 shade depth per color area (base, shadow, highlight)
- Slight dithering for gradients only in larger areas
- Crisp rendering at all display sizes (image-rendering: pixelated)

**Display sizes**:
- Small (icons, badges): 32px rendered, 16x16 source
- Standard (game hub): 128-192px rendered, 48x48 source
- Large (special moments): 256px+ rendered, 48x48 source with detail pass

### 4.11 SVG Direction

The mascot should be rendered as SVG (like the current system) for:
- Crisp pixel rendering at any size
- Programmatic animation control
- Color palette swapping
- No external image dependencies
- Small file size

The SVG approach should evolve to support:
- Named animation frames (frame arrays, not bitmap strings)
- Expression layers (base body + expression overlay)
- Accessory layers (collars, scarves, seasonal items)
- Particle attachment points (for hearts, sparkles, ZZZs)

### 4.12 Future Scalability

The mascot system must support:

- **Multiple species** (future): Dog, rabbit, bird — same skeleton, different sprite
- **Customization**: Color palettes, accessories, patterns
- **Evolution stages**: Same character, different proportions and details
- **Seasonal variants**: Hats, scarves, themed accessories
- **Personality visual traits**: Slight variations based on care patterns
- **Emotional states**: 6-8 expression sets that combine with body poses

## 5. UI Evolution Strategy

### 5.1 Landing Page

**Current state**: Mesh gradient background, floating pixel cats, bento grid, GSAP animations. Fun but unfocused.

**Evolution direction**: The landing page should feel like the cover of a storybook. One dominant image of Scan Chan (the mascot) in a warm, sunlit room. The headline communicates the emotional promise, not features. The CTA is a single, inviting button.

**Emotional flow**: Curiosity → Warmth → Invitation

**Hierarchy**:
1. Scan Chan (mascot) — largest element, centered
2. Emotional headline — "Your companion is waiting"
3. Single CTA — "Begin your story" (warm, rounded)
4. Subtle secondary link — "Learn more"

**What to remove**: Floating cats (distract from the mascot), bento grid (too information-dense), excessive parallax (too busy).

### 5.2 Game Hub

**Current state**: Tab-based layout with missions, stats, history, achievements, products. Data-heavy. The pet is small and peripheral.

**Evolution direction**: The Game Hub becomes **the room**. Scan Chan occupies the center. Stats are ambient visual cues on the pet, not data panels. Tabs become a gentle bottom navigation. The room itself tells you everything — food bowl, toys, window light.

**Emotional flow**: Homecoming → Attunement → Care → Satisfaction

**Hierarchy**:
1. Scan Chan (center, largest)
2. Room environment (context, atmosphere)
3. Quick actions (feed, scan — bottom)
4. Navigation (gentle, minimal — bottom or side)
5. Stat indicators (ambient, on or near pet)

**What to remove**: Tab-heavy navigation, data grids, statistics panels as primary content, any element that competes with the pet.

### 5.3 Scanner

**Current state**: Full-screen camera with overlay. Functional but disconnected from the pet experience.

**Evolution direction**: The scanner should feel like the cat's "hunting mode." The pet watches from the corner as you scan. The scan result triggers a feeding animation. The transition into and out of the scanner should feel organic — like opening a window in the room.

**Emotional flow**: Focus → Anticipation → Discovery → Delight

### 5.4 Profile

**Current state**: Nickname setup, avatar selection. Basic.

**Evolution direction**: The profile becomes a "journal cover." The player's name, their cat's name, how long they've been together, and a small portrait. It should feel like the inside cover of a personal diary.

### 5.5 Achievements

**Current state**: List view with badges. Functional.

**Evolution direction**: Achievements become a "trophy shelf" in the room — or a dedicated shelf/album that the player can browse. Each achievement is a small pixel-art vignette, not just a badge. Unlocking an achievement should feel like receiving a gift.

### 5.6 Inventory / Products

**Current state**: Tabular product list with search. Database-like.

**Evolution direction**: Products become a "pantry" or "scrapbook." Each product is a visual card with the pixel-art representation. The collection should feel like a personal museum of everything you've shared with your cat.

### 5.7 Future Pet Room

The room is the stage for the pet's life. It should feel like a real space:
- Clear floor area where the cat walks
- Walls with shelves, window, decorations
- Furniture that the cat interacts with (sits on, sleeps near, plays with)
- Time-of-day lighting that shifts naturally
- Seasonal decorations that change with the calendar

### 5.8 Future Furniture

Each furniture piece should:
- Match the pixel-art style of the pet
- Have a clear pixel-art sprite (32x32 or 64x64)
- Cast a subtle shadow consistent with room lighting
- Have an idle animation if appropriate (swinging, rotating)
- Be interacted with by the cat (sit, sleep, play)

### 5.9 Future Collection

The collection should be a **scrapbook** — not a database. Pages turn. Items are arranged visually, not in rows. The experience of browsing the collection should feel like looking through a photo album.

## 6. Preparation for Character Bible

### 6.1 Recommendations for the Character Design Phase

These observations and decisions should inform the future Character Design Document:

**The mascot must be designed as a system, not a single image.**
It needs:
- A base body sprite per evolution stage
- An expression layer system (at least 8 expressions)
- A pose system (sitting, standing, walking, sleeping, stretching, playing)
- An accessory layer system (collars, hats, seasonal items)
- A particle attachment system (hearts, sparkles, ZZZs, steam)

**The pixel grid must be finalized before any sprite work begins.**
The choice between 32x32 and 48x48 affects every downstream decision. Recommendation: 48x48 for the base resolution, displayed at various sizes. This provides enough detail for expressive eyes while remaining clearly pixel-art.

**The color palette per stage must be defined before sprite creation.**
Each evolution stage should have a distinct palette that communicates growth:
- Kitten: Lighter, softer, pastel-leaning
- Young Cat: Slightly more saturated
- Adult Cat: Full saturation, distinct markings
- Wise Cat: Slightly desaturated, silver/gray accents
- Legendary Cat: Base palette + golden/ethereal overlay

**The personality system must be mapped to visual traits before implementation.**
How does a "Foodie" cat look different from an "Adventurous" cat? These distinctions should be subtle but visible — perhaps slight marking differences, accessory preferences, or idle animation frequency.

**Expression design should start with the eyes.**
The eyes should be designed as a standalone expression sheet before any body sprites are created. If the eyes work, everything else will follow.

**Animation priority order**:
1. Idle breathing (always running)
2. Blinking (random interval)
3. Tail movement (continuous, mood-based)
4. Greeting (when player opens app)
5. Feeding reaction (when scanned product appears)
6. Sleeping (long-duration idle)
7. Evolution sequence (one-time, high-impact)

**The mascot must be tested at multiple sizes before finalization.**
A sprite that looks great at 192px may be illegible at 32px. The design must work at:
- 32px (badge/icon)
- 64px (small companion)
- 128px (standard hub view)
- 256px (special moments)

**The silhouette test is critical.**
Print the mascot in solid black. If the shape is not immediately recognizable as "cute cat," the silhouette needs work. The ears, tail, and body proportions must be distinctive.

### 6.2 Open Questions for the Character Bible

These questions must be answered during the character design phase:

10. What is the evolution transition visual? (Cross-fade, glow, full sequence)

### 6.3 Design Constraints

These constraints must be respected during character design:

- **Must be pixel art** — consistent with the Cozy Pixel Realism visual direction
- **Must work at small sizes** — readable at 32px display
- **Must be SVG-renderable** — no external image files
- **Must support 5 evolution stages** — same character, different proportions
- **Must have expressive eyes** — the soul of the character
- **Must be warm-colored** — aligned with the Honey/Cream palette
- **Must feel like a cat** — not a cartoon character, not a mascot, a cat
- **Must never look sad or distressed** — only mild grumpiness at lowest stats
- **Must be animatable** — every design decision must consider movement
