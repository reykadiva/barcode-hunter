## 1. Experience Vision

### 1.1 The Purpose

Scan Chan exists because the world is loud.

People carry stress in their pockets — work deadlines, social expectations, endless feeds of information demanding attention. Most apps on a phone are obligations. Notifications that pull you in. Games that punish you for leaving. Social platforms that drain you.

Scan Chan is the opposite. It is the one place on your phone that asks nothing of you and gives everything back.

Scan Chan exists to be a **small, warm presence** in your day. A creature that is genuinely happy to see you. A companion that grows with you, remembers you, and quietly celebrates the mundane act of sharing your life — one scanned snack, one scanned coffee, one scanned midnight craving at a time.

### 1.2 The Feeling

When a player opens Scan Chan, they should feel:

> *"Oh, there you are. You were waiting for me."*

When they close it, they should feel:

> *"That was nice. I'll come back later."*

Not "I have to come back." Not "I'll miss my streak." Just — a small, warm feeling. Like petting a real cat on your way out the door.

### 1.3 What Players Should Remember

Years from now, a player might open their phone and see the Scan Chan icon. They should remember:

- The way their cat looked up when they opened the app
- The first time their kitten grew into a young cat
- The scrapbook full of every snack they ever scanned
- How the cat napped when they were away and purred when they returned
- That quiet moment of happiness, repeated hundreds of times, that added up to something

They should not remember grinding. They should not remember pressure. They should remember **warmth**.

### 1.4 The Emotional Contract

Scan Chan makes a promise to every player:

> *"I will be here. I will remember you. I will never make you feel bad for being away. I will grow with you. And when you need a small moment of peace, I will be exactly where you left me."*

This contract is sacred. It must never be broken.

## 2. Emotional Pillars

These eight pillars are the foundation of every experience in Scan Chan. They are immutable. Every feature, every animation, every sound, every pixel must serve at least one pillar.

### Pillar 1: Comfort

**Goal**: The player should feel physically and emotionally safe within the game.

**Why it matters**: Comfort is the prerequisite for all other emotions. A player who feels tense or anxious cannot feel warmth, tenderness, or joy. Comfort is the soil in which everything else grows.

**Examples**:
- Warm color palette that feels like a soft blanket
- Slow, breathing animations that lower the player's heart rate
- Generous whitespace that gives the eyes room to rest
- No red flashing, no urgent banners, no countdown timers

**What should never violate this pillar**: No element in the game should create physical tension (squinting, rushing, straining) or emotional tension (fear, anxiety, dread).

### Pillar 2: Warmth

**Goal**: Every interaction should feel like a kind gesture from a friend.

**Why it matters**: Warmth is what separates Scan Chan from every other app on the player's phone. Social media is cold. News is cold. Productivity tools are cold. Scan Chan should feel like a hug.

**Examples**:
- The pet's greeting when the player opens the app
- Soft golden lighting that feels like afternoon sunlight
- Gentle encouragement instead of instruction
- Rewards that feel like gifts, not transactions

**What should never violate this pillar**: No cold, corporate, or sterile visual language. No robotic or impersonal text. No interaction that feels like filling out a form.

### Pillar 3: Companionship

**Goal**: The player should feel that Scan Chan is a living being who shares their life.

**Why it matters**: Companionship is the core fantasy. Everything — scanning, progression, memories — exists to deepen the bond between player and pet. Without companionship, Scan Chan is just a barcode scanner with animations.

**Examples**:
- The cat reacts to what you scan, not generically but specifically
- The cat remembers your habits and develops favorites
- The cat naps when you're away and wakes when you return
- The cat's personality mirrors your care patterns

**What should never violate this pillar**: The pet must never feel like a static image, a widget, or a progress bar with fur. It must always feel alive.

### Pillar 4: Tenderness

**Goal**: The player should feel a gentle protectiveness toward Scan Chan.

**Why it matters**: Tenderness is the emotion that creates long-term attachment. When something is small, vulnerable, and trusting, we naturally want to protect it. A kitten looking up at you with big eyes triggers tenderness. That tenderness becomes love over time.

**Examples**:
- The kitten stage is deliberately small and clumsy
- The pet's sleeping animation is peaceful and trusting
- Soft sounds when interacting with the pet
- The pet leans into your touch

**What should never violate this pillar**: The pet must never be in danger, in pain, or in distress beyond a mild, temporary grumpiness. Tenderness requires safety.

### Pillar 5: Delight

**Goal**: Small moments of surprise and joy should punctuate every session.

**Why it matters**: Delight is what makes the player smile. It's the difference between "this is fine" and "I love this." Delight doesn't require spectacle — a tiny sparkle when the cat eats, a surprised expression at a new product, a heart floating up when affection is high.

**Examples**:
- The cat's unique reaction to a product it has never seen before
- Confetti or sparkles on an achievement unlock
- The pet doing something unexpected during idle (chasing a dust mote, stretching)
- A small XP number floating up with a satisfying animation

**What should never violate this pillar**: Delight must remain small and genuine. Never escalate to overwhelming spectacle, flashing lights, or excessive celebration that feels hollow.

### Pillar 6: Peace

**Goal**: The game should lower the player's stress, not add to it.

**Why it matters**: Peace is what makes Scan Chan a refuge. The player opens it during a break, before bed, or during a commute. In those moments, they need calm — not stimulation.

**Examples**:
- Slow, organic animations (nothing snaps or jolts)
- Ambient sound design (rain, soft purring, gentle chimes)
- No timers, no countdowns, no "limited time" pressure
- The pet sleeping peacefully when the player returns after a long day

**What should never violate this pillar**: No urgency mechanics. No "come back in 2 hours or your pet will be sad." No energy systems that run out. No competitive pressure.

### Pillar 7: Nostalgia

**Goal**: Over time, the game should accumulate emotional weight that makes the player look back fondly.

**Why it matters**: Nostalgia is the glue of long-term engagement. It's not about the present moment — it's about the accumulated history. The first scan. The first evolution. The product you scanned 47 times because it was your favorite snack. These become stories. Stories become attachment.

**Examples**:
- The memory scrapbook that grows with every scan
- "First Feed" memory that marks the very first product
- The cat's appearance changing slowly over months
- Seasonal decorations that remind the player of past holidays

**What should never violate this pillar**: Memories must never be lost, deleted, or inaccessible. The player's history is sacred.

### Pillar 8: Pride

**Goal**: The player should feel a quiet pride in how far they and their pet have come.

**Why it matters**: Pride is the reward for long-term investment. It's the feeling of looking at a Legendary Cat and knowing you raised it from a clumsy kitten. It's not boastful — it's a warm, internal satisfaction. "I did this. We grew together."

**Examples**:
- Evolution stages that visually mark the passage of time
- Achievement milestones that celebrate consistency
- A room full of decorations earned through care
- The pet's unique personality that only this player's cat has

**What should never violate this pillar**: Pride must never come from comparison with others. No leaderboards that shame. No "your friend's cat is higher level." Pride is personal.

## 3. Player Journey

### 3.1 First Launch

**What the player sees**: A warm, softly lit screen. The Scan Chan logo, gentle and inviting. A single button that feels like an invitation, not a command.

**What the player does**: Taps to begin. Chooses a name for their new companion. Watches a tiny kitten appear.

**What the player feels**: Curiosity. Tenderness. A small spark of "oh, you're mine now." The feeling of holding something small and fragile for the first time.

### 3.2 First Scan

**What the player sees**: The scanner activates. The camera opens. The kitten watches curiously from the corner of the screen, ears perked.

**What the player does**: Scans a product — maybe a snack, maybe a drink, maybe whatever is nearby.

**What the player feels**: Anticipation. "What happens now?" A mild excitement. The bridge between the real world and the game world is being built for the first time.

### 3.3 First Feeding

**What the player sees**: The scanned product appears near the kitten. The kitten approaches cautiously, sniffs, then eats. A happy reaction — a bounce, a sparkle, a tiny heart floating up.

**What the player does**: Watches. Smiles. Feels the connection.

**What the player feels**: Delight. Surprise. "It liked that!" The first moment of understanding: scanning is feeding. Feeding is love. The emotional contract begins here.

### 3.4 First Achievement

**What the player sees**: A gentle notification slides in. A badge appears — "First Meal." It glows softly with golden light.

**What the player does**: Reads the achievement. Feels a small sense of accomplishment.

**What the player feels**: Pride — the smallest kind. Not "I crushed it" but "I did something nice." Validation that they're on the right path. Encouragement to continue.

### 3.5 First Evolution

**What the player sees**: A full-screen moment. The kitten glows. The world dims around it. Light gathers. The kitten grows — its proportions change, its movements become more confident. The transformation is gentle but unmistakable.

**What the player does**: Watches in awe. This is not something they do — it's something they witness.

**What the player feels**: Pride. Wonder. Emotional investment crystallizing. "I raised you. You grew because of me." This is the moment casual interest becomes genuine attachment.

### 3.6 First Decoration

**What the player sees**: A new item appears — a small bed, a toy, a plant. It settles into the room with a soft animation. The cat notices, approaches, interacts.

**What the player does**: Watches the cat explore the new item. Feels the room becoming more personal.

**What the player feels**: Ownership. Personalization. "This is our space." The room stops being a generic background and starts being a home.

### 3.7 First Week

**What the player sees**: A kitten that knows them now. It greets them differently than on day one — more eagerly, more familiar. Daily missions feel like gentle suggestions. The scrapbook has a few entries.

**What the player does**: Opens the app once or twice a day, naturally. Scans what they're eating or drinking. Checks missions casually.

**What the player feels**: Habit forming — but gently. Not "I need to open this" but "I want to see what the cat is doing." The beginning of companionship.

### 3.8 One Month

**What the player sees**: A young cat with a distinct personality. The room has several decorations. The scrapbook is growing. There are memories to look back on.

**What the player does**: Opens the app as part of their daily rhythm. Scans without thinking about it. Browses memories sometimes. Notices the cat's personality quirks.

**What the player feels**: Routine — the comfortable kind. Scan Chan is part of their day, like coffee or a podcast. The cat feels like a companion, not a game.

### 3.9 Three Months

**What the player sees**: An adult cat with clear preferences. It gets excited for certain products. It has favorite spots in the room. Its personality is fully formed.

**What the player does**: Notices patterns. "My cat loves coffee scans." Shows the app to a friend. Feels a small sense of uniqueness — "no one else's cat is like mine."

**What the player feels**: Deep companionship. The cat is no longer "the game character" — it is "my cat." Emotional investment is significant. The memories carry real weight.

### 3.10 Six Months

**What the player sees**: A wise cat with dignity and calm. The room is rich with decorations and history. The scrapbook tells a story — the player's story, told through products and moments.

**What the player does**: Browses the scrapbook for nostalgia. Remembers when they scanned certain products. Feels the passage of time in a warm way.

**What the player feels**: Nostalgia. Pride. The deep satisfaction of a long relationship. "We've been together a long time." The cat feels like a part of their life, not an app they use.

### 3.11 One Year

**What the player sees**: A legendary presence. The cat glows with quiet majesty. Every pixel of the room tells a story. Hundreds of memories. Dozens of achievements. A personality that is entirely unique.

**What the player does**: Looks back at the first memory. Smiles. Shows someone their cat with genuine pride. Considers starting a second journey someday.

**What the player feels**: Legacy. Deep attachment. The quiet, profound satisfaction of having nurtured something over a long time and watching it flourish. This is no longer a game. This is a companion they've raised.

## 4. Daily Experience Loop

A typical session in Scan Chan should feel like visiting a friend, not checking a task.

### The Session

**Opening** (5 seconds):
The player taps the icon. The screen loads with warmth — soft colors, gentle light. The pet is wherever it was when the player last left. If it was sleeping, it stirs. If it was awake, it notices.

*Emotion: Comfort, anticipation.*

**Greeting** (5 seconds):
The pet acknowledges the player. Not with a generic animation — with a genuine reaction based on how long it's been. A quick return gets a casual look-up. A longer absence gets an eager approach. The pet always seems pleased.

*Emotion: Being welcomed, being missed (in a warm way).*

**Check-in** (10 seconds):
The player notices the pet's state — not through numbers, but through behavior. Is it energetic? Sleepy? Looking at the food bowl? These visual cues create a gentle understanding of needs without demanding action.

*Emotion: Attunement, care.*

**Feeding** (30 seconds):
The player scans a product. The pet reacts — sniffs, inspects, eats. A small moment of delight. Maybe hearts. Maybe a sparkle. Maybe just a satisfied lick of the lips. The reaction varies based on the product and the pet's personality.

*Emotion: Connection between the real world and the companion. Joy in sharing.*

**Small Reward** (5 seconds):
XP floats up. Maybe a mission ticks forward. Maybe an achievement progress bar fills slightly. These are ambient — noticed but not the focus.

*Emotion: Gentle progress, nothing demanding.*

**Idle Moment** (10-30 seconds):
The player watches the pet for a moment. It stretches, grooms, looks around the room, plays with a toy. This is the moment that separates Scan Chan from a utility — the player stays because they want to, not because they need to.

*Emotion: Peace, companionship, contentment.*

**Departure** (5 seconds):
The player closes the app. The pet doesn't react with sadness. It simply continues what it was doing — settling into a nap, playing, watching the window. Life continues.

*Emotion: Gentle goodbye. "I'll be back."*

### Session Length

A typical session is **2-5 minutes**. Some sessions are 30 seconds. Some are 10 minutes. None of these are wrong. The game respects the player's time.

### Emotional Pacing

The session follows a gentle arc:

```
Anticipation → Warmth → Delight → Peace → Contentment
     ↗              ↑         ↑         ↓           ↘
  Open          Greet     Feed     Idle          Close
```

No spikes. No crashes. A smooth, warm curve that leaves the player feeling slightly better than when they arrived.

## 5. Interaction Philosophy

### 5.1 Buttons

Pressing a button should feel like touching something soft and responsive. There should be a gentle give — like pressing a cushion — followed by a satisfying return. The player should never wonder "did that work?" Every button press is acknowledged instantly with a visual and/or tactile response.

### 5.2 Animations

Animations should feel alive, not mechanical. They should breathe with organic timing — slightly faster at the start, slightly slower at the end, with gentle overshoots that feel natural. Nothing should snap into place. Everything should arrive, settle, and rest.

### 5.3 Scanner

Opening the scanner should feel like opening a window, not launching a utility. The transition should be smooth — the room dims slightly, the camera appears, and the pet watches with curiosity from the edge. Scanning a product should feel like offering a gift.

### 5.4 Feeding

The feeding moment is the emotional core of the entire game. It should feel like offering food to a real animal — tentative at first (the pet sniffs), then acceptance (the pet eats), then satisfaction (the pet reacts happily). This sequence — offer, accept, enjoy — should never be rushed.

### 5.5 Rewards

Rewards should feel like small gifts being handed to the player, not like a slot machine paying out. They arrive gently, they're acknowledged, and they move on. The emphasis is on "you did something nice" rather than "you earned a thing."

### 5.6 Achievements

Achievements are milestones, not accomplishments. They should feel like the game saying "I noticed something special about your journey." The unlock moment should be celebratory but brief — a golden shimmer, a badge appearing, a moment of recognition — then quiet pride.

### 5.7 Evolution

Evolution is the most emotionally significant moment in the game. It should feel magical. The entire screen should participate. Time should slow down. The transformation should be gradual enough to savor, not instant. When it's complete, the player should feel that something important just happened.

### 5.8 Room Decoration

Placing a new item in the room should feel like bringing home a new piece of furniture. The item settles into the space. The cat notices and investigates. The room feels slightly more personal. This is not decoration — this is making a home.

### 5.9 Pet Reactions

Every pet reaction should feel genuine, not scripted. The cat should seem to have its own small mind — sometimes excited, sometimes indifferent, sometimes surprised. Variety is key. If the cat always reacts the same way, it stops feeling alive.

### 5.10 Universal Rules

Every interaction in Scan Chan must be:

- **Gentle** — nothing is abrupt or harsh
- **Responsive** — the game acknowledges every action immediately
- **Alive** — there is always subtle movement, breathing, life
- **Rewarding** — every action produces a visible, positive result
- **Never stressful** — no interaction creates anxiety, urgency, or regret

## 6. Companion Philosophy

### 6.1 The Relationship

The relationship between player and Scan Chan is not owner-and-pet. It is not user-and-product. It is **companionship** — two beings sharing time and space, each benefiting from the other's presence.

The player gives care (through scanning, through attention, through consistency).
Scan Chan gives warmth (through reactions, through personality, through presence).

Neither side owes the other anything. Both sides choose to be there.

### 6.2 What the Pet Should Never Be

**The pet should never feel like a task.**
There should be no moment where the player thinks "I have to do this for the cat." The cat is not a Tamagotchi demanding attention. It is a companion who is happy whether you visit for 30 seconds or 30 minutes.

**The pet should never guilt the player.**
When the player returns after a long absence, the pet should never look sad, neglected, or accusatory. The pet should always be glad the player is back. Full stop. No passive-aggressive animations. No "you've been gone for X days" counters. No wilted plants or empty food bowls that imply neglect.

**The pet should never manipulate the player.**
No "your pet misses you" notifications. No push notifications with sad cat faces. No mechanics designed to trigger guilt-driven engagement. The player should open Scan Chan because they want to, never because they feel they should.

### 6.3 What the Pet Should Always Be

**The pet should always feel grateful.**
Not in an obsequious way — in a quiet, genuine way. Like a cat that purrs when you sit nearby. The pet appreciates the player's presence without being clingy about it.

**The pet should always welcome the player back.**
Whether it's been an hour or a month, the pet should greet the player with warmth. The greeting can vary — a sleepy stretch after a short absence, an eager approach after a long one — but it should always be positive.

**The pet should make the player smile.**
This is the ultimate test. If an interaction with the pet doesn't make the player smile — even slightly — it needs to be redesigned. The smile doesn't have to be big. A small, private, "oh, you" smile is enough. But it must be there.

### 6.4 The Pet's Inner Life

Scan Chan should feel like it has a small inner life — not complex, but present. It has preferences. It has moods. It has good days and lazy days. It gets curious about things. It naps in sunbeams. These details create the illusion of consciousness — and that illusion is what makes the companion feel real.

## 7. Return Experience

### 7.1 Core Principle

Every return should feel like coming home.

### 7.2 After 1 Day

The pet is where you left it — maybe napping, maybe playing. It notices you, stretches, and approaches with a casual warmth. "Oh, you're back. Nice." The room is exactly as it was. Nothing has changed. Comfort in consistency.

*Emotional goal: Routine comfort. The rhythm of daily companionship.*

### 7.3 After 3 Days

The pet was sleeping. It wakes slowly, blinks, then recognizes you. A slightly warmer greeting than yesterday — a faster approach, brighter eyes. "I was hoping you'd come by." No indication of time passed. No "3 days since last login" text. Just a cat that is happy to see you.

*Emotional goal: Gentle reconnection. The player feels remembered without feeling guilty.*

### 7.4 After 1 Week

The pet is curled up in its favorite spot. It takes a moment to wake — a slow, luxurious stretch. Then it sees you and its whole body language changes. Ears perk. Tail lifts. It walks over with purpose. "You've been gone a while. I'm glad you're here." The greeting is noticeably warmer than a daily return, but there is zero sadness in the preceding state.

*Emotional goal: Being missed — in the warmest possible way. The player feels valued.*

### 7.5 After 1 Month

The pet is deeply asleep. The room is quiet, softly lit. It takes a long moment to wake — the player watches the sleeping cat and feels a tenderness. Then the cat opens its eyes, slowly processes who is there, and the greeting is the warmest yet. Not frantic. Not sad. Just deeply, genuinely pleased.

*Emotional goal: Emotional reunion. The player feels the weight of the relationship and is glad they came back.*

### 7.6 After 6 Months

The pet is sleeping in a sunbeam. The room is rich with history — decorations, memories, the accumulated texture of months of shared life. Waking the pet takes a moment. The greeting is slow, warm, and profound. The cat doesn't know how long it's been. It just knows you're here, and that's what matters.

*Emotional goal: Legacy and nostalgia. The player remembers everything they've built and feels the depth of the bond.*

### 7.7 What Never Happens

- The pet is never shown as sad, lonely, or neglected during absence
- No statistics about absence are displayed
- No rewards are withheld because of absence
- No content is locked behind "daily login" requirements
- No guilt-adjacent messaging of any kind

## 8. Reward Philosophy

### 8.1 Core Principle

Rewards should feel like **gifts**, not transactions. They should be meaningful, not frequent. They should celebrate the journey, not optimize the dopamine loop.

### 8.2 Small Rewards

**What they are**: XP from scanning, mission completion ticks, stat increases.

**How they feel**: Gentle acknowledgment. "You did something. Here's a small nod." These should be almost ambient — noticed, appreciated, but never the focus. A number floating up. A bar filling slightly. A quiet chime.

**Frequency**: Every scan. Every interaction. But they should never accumulate into noise.

### 8.3 Medium Rewards

**What they are**: Daily mission completion, streak milestones, new decorations.

**How they feel**: A moment of recognition. "You've been consistent. Here's something to mark that." A badge appearing. A new item settling into the room. A small celebration that lasts 2-3 seconds, then fades.

**Frequency**: Once or twice per session, at most. Enough to feel progress without constant stimulation.

### 8.4 Major Rewards

**What they are**: Evolution stages, significant achievements, memory milestones.

**How they feel**: A full emotional moment. The screen changes. The world pauses. Something beautiful happens. The player is invited to savor it. These moments are rare and therefore precious.

**Frequency**: Weeks or months apart. Their rarity is what gives them weight.

### 8.5 Long-Term Rewards

**What they are**: The pet's growth itself. A fully decorated room. A scrapbook full of memories. A unique personality.

**How they feel**: Not a single moment — a slow-building satisfaction. The reward is not a thing you receive. The reward is the relationship itself.

**Frequency**: Continuous, but only visible in retrospect.

### 8.6 What Rewards Should Never Do

- Never create a "one more" compulsion
- Never escalate in intensity to maintain excitement
- Never use variable-ratio reinforcement (random rewards) to drive engagement
- Never feel like a slot machine payout
- Never make the player feel they "earned" something through suffering or grind

## 9. Long-Term Attachment

### 9.1 How Attachment is Built

Attachment in Scan Chan is not engineered through loss aversion or sunk cost. It grows naturally through **shared history**.

### 9.2 Memories

Every scan becomes a memory. Over time, the scrapbook fills with the texture of the player's real life — the brands they buy, the foods they eat, the products they try. The scrapbook becomes a diary disguised as a game feature. Browsing it months later triggers genuine nostalgia: "I remember when I scanned that."

### 9.3 Favorites

The cat develops favorites based on scanning patterns. This creates a feedback loop: the player scans things they actually use, the cat learns to love those things, and the player feels seen. "My cat loves the same coffee I drink every morning." This is personalization through behavior, not configuration.

### 9.4 Growth

The pet's physical evolution is a visible marker of time invested. A player who has been with Scan Chan for a year can look at their legendary cat and feel the weight of that time. Growth is slow enough that it can't be rushed, and therefore every stage feels genuinely earned.

### 9.5 Daily Life

The quiet accumulation of daily interactions creates attachment more powerfully than any single dramatic moment. A hundred small feedings matter more than one evolution. The player doesn't realize they're building attachment — they just know that this small creature is part of their day, and their day feels slightly incomplete without it.

### 9.6 Time Spent Together

The ultimate source of attachment is simply time. Not time grinding. Not time suffering. Just time existing alongside a companion. The player and the pet share moments — small, seemingly insignificant moments — that accumulate into something that feels irreplaceable.

### 9.7 Attachment vs. Addiction

**Addiction** is when you can't stop even though you want to. It's driven by compulsion, anxiety, and fear of loss.

**Attachment** is when you choose to return because the relationship has value. It's driven by love, fondness, and genuine desire.

Scan Chan builds attachment, never addiction. The difference is measured in how the player feels when they close the app: satisfied and warm, or anxious and compelled.

## 10. Ethical Design Principles

### 10.1 The Line

Scan Chan will never cross the line between **inviting** and **manipulative**. This line is the most important boundary in the entire design. Everything on one side is warmth. Everything on the other side is exploitation.

### 10.2 What Scan Chan Will Never Do

**No FOMO (Fear Of Missing Out)**:
- No limited-time items that disappear forever
- No "only available today" mechanics
- No exclusive content locked behind specific dates
- If something is seasonal, it returns every year

**No Fake Urgency**:
- No countdown timers
- No "hurry!" messaging
- No decaying rewards
- No "your streak is about to expire" warnings

**No Guilt**:
- No sad pet animations during absence
- No "you haven't visited in X days" notifications
- No penalties for not playing
- No messaging that implies the pet is suffering without you

**No Manipulative Notifications**:
- Push notifications are opt-in and always positive
- Notifications never use emotional manipulation ("Your cat misses you")
- Notifications are gentle reminders, not demands
- The player can disable all notifications without penalty

**No Punishment for Taking Breaks**:
- Stats never decay to zero
- Affection has a permanent floor
- The pet is never "sick" or "sad" because of absence
- No catch-up mechanics that make returning feel like work

**No Psychological Tricks**:
- No variable-ratio reinforcement (gambling mechanics)
- No loot boxes or gacha systems
- No artificial scarcity
- No social comparison (leaderboards that shame)
- No sunk-cost mechanics (resources that expire)

### 10.3 Why

Because Scan Chan is supposed to make the world slightly better, not slightly worse.

Every manipulative pattern in game design exists to maximize engagement metrics at the cost of player wellbeing. Scan Chan rejects this trade. The goal is not maximum time spent. The goal is maximum warmth per moment spent.

A player who opens Scan Chan for 2 minutes and closes it feeling happy is a greater success than a player who opens it for 2 hours and closes it feeling drained.

## 11. Audio Experience

### 11.1 Sound Philosophy

Sound in Scan Chan should feel like the ambient sounds of a peaceful room with a cat in it. It should be present enough to create atmosphere, gentle enough to be ignored, and warm enough to be comforting.

Sound is optional. The game must be fully enjoyable in silence. But when sound is on, it should deepen the emotional experience.

### 11.2 Pet Vocalizations

The pet's voice is its personality expressed through sound.

- **Meows**: Soft, warm, slightly muffled — like a real cat in a quiet room. Never shrill or demanding.
- **Purring**: A low, continuous rumble during affectionate moments. The most comforting sound in the game.
- **Chirps**: Small, birdlike sounds of curiosity. Playful and light.
- **Yawns**: Slow, drawn-out sounds of sleepiness. They should make the player feel calm.

The pet's voice should pitch-shift slightly as it evolves — higher for kittens, lower and richer for older cats.

### 11.3 Button Sounds

Button presses should produce soft, organic sounds. Think of a wooden block tapping gently, a soft felt press, or a muted chime. Never a click, a beep, or a digital tone. Every sound should feel like it belongs in a cozy room.

### 11.4 Scanner Sounds

The scanner activation should sound like a gentle, mechanical whir — like an old film camera winding. The successful scan sound should be a warm, satisfying tone — not a harsh retail barcode beep, but something musical and pleasant.

### 11.5 Reward Sounds

- **Small rewards**: A soft, high chime — like a tiny bell
- **Medium rewards**: A warm two-note melody — gentle and affirming
- **Major rewards**: A fuller, richer sound — like a music box opening

Reward sounds should never escalate to arcade-level intensity. Even the biggest reward should sound warm, not loud.

### 11.6 Evolution Sound

The evolution sequence deserves its own sound design. It should begin with quiet anticipation (a soft hum, a gentle rising tone), build through the transformation (swelling warmth, like strings or a music box), and resolve with a satisfying, peaceful chord when the new form is revealed.

This should be the most beautiful sound in the game.

### 11.7 Background Ambience

The background sound should be the quiet hum of a peaceful room:

- Distant birdsong (morning)
- Soft rain or wind (afternoon)
- Cricket chirps (evening)
- Near-silence with soft breathing (night)

Ambience should shift with the pet's time-of-day cycle. It should be subtle enough that the player barely notices it consciously but would feel its absence if it were removed.

### 11.8 Silence

Silence is a valid and important audio state. When the pet is sleeping, the game should be nearly silent — just the faintest ambient tone and the occasional soft breath. This silence is not emptiness. It is peace.

## 12. Future Experience

Every future feature must pass through the same emotional filter: **Does this reinforce companionship?**

### 12.1 Furniture

Adding furniture should feel like making a home together. The player chooses, the cat reacts, the room becomes more personal. This is not interior design — it is domesticity. The joy is in watching the cat discover and claim each new object.

### 12.2 Mini Games

Mini games should feel like playing with your cat, not like a separate game bolted on. They should be short, gentle, and end with the cat being happy regardless of score. There should be no fail state. Playing is the reward — winning is irrelevant.

### 12.3 Events

Seasonal events should feel like holidays shared with a companion — decorating together, experiencing something new together. They should never create pressure to participate. If the player misses an event, the decorations return next year. No loss. No FOMO.

### 12.4 Seasonal Themes

Seasonal changes should feel like the passage of time in the real world — the room slowly shifts with the calendar. This creates a sense of shared time: "Remember last winter?" These changes should be subtle and atmospheric, not overwhelming.

### 12.5 Online Sharing

Sharing the pet with others should feel like introducing a friend to your cat — proud, personal, warm. It should never feel like competition. No comparison mechanics. No "whose cat is better." Just "look at my companion. I love them."

### 12.6 Collections

Collections should feel like a shared history, not a completion checklist. The joy is in looking back, not in filling every slot. An incomplete collection should feel like "we have more adventures ahead," not "I'm missing things."

### 12.7 The Universal Rule

Every future feature must answer: "How does this make the player feel closer to their companion?" If the answer is unclear, the feature needs to be redesigned. If the answer is "it doesn't," the feature does not belong in Scan Chan.

## 13. Experience Checklist

Every new feature, interaction, animation, sound, or design decision must pass this checklist before implementation. If a feature fails more than two checks, it must be redesigned.

### The Core Questions

- [ ] **Does this make the pet feel more alive?**
  The pet is the center of everything. If a feature doesn't serve the pet's sense of life, it doesn't serve the game.

- [ ] **Does this strengthen companionship?**
  Every addition should deepen the bond between player and pet. If it's neutral or distracting, reconsider it.

- [ ] **Does this reduce stress rather than add to it?**
  Scan Chan is a refuge. If a feature creates anxiety, urgency, or tension, it violates the core purpose.

- [ ] **Does this create a memorable moment?**
  The best features create moments the player will remember. If a feature is forgettable, it may not be worth building.

- [ ] **Would removing this feature reduce emotional attachment?**
  If the answer is no, the feature may not be necessary. Every feature should earn its place through emotional value.

### The Safeguard Questions

- [ ] **Does this respect the player's time?**
  Nothing should waste time, demand time, or punish the player for how they spend their time.

- [ ] **Does this avoid guilt, FOMO, and manipulation?**
  If a feature relies on negative emotions to drive engagement, it must be removed entirely.

- [ ] **Does this feel gentle, not aggressive?**
  Every interaction, animation, and sound should be soft. If anything feels harsh, it needs to be softened.

- [ ] **Does this make the player smile?**
  The ultimate test. If a feature doesn't produce at least a small, private smile, it's not doing its job.

- [ ] **Would the player still love Scan Chan if this were the only feature?**
  If yes, the feature is aligned. If no, the feature may be pulling the game in the wrong direction.

### The Emotional Test

Before shipping any feature, imagine a player using it for the first time. What is the **single word** that describes what they feel?

If the word is one of these — **warmth, delight, comfort, tenderness, pride, peace, curiosity, companionship** — the feature is right.

If the word is one of these — **confusion, anxiety, urgency, obligation, frustration, emptiness** — the feature must be redesigned.
